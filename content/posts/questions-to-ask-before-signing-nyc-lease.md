# 11 Questions to Ask Before Signing a NYC Lease

**Before signing a NYC lease, confirm the upfront cost matches the legal limits: only a security deposit (capped at one month), the first month of rent, and any application fee (capped at $20). Clarify who pays the broker fee, whether the unit is rent stabilized, who covers utilities, and that you will be a named tenant on a lease you can fully review.**

Signing a lease is a calm, deliberate act, not a race. Research has refuted the myth that units rent in hours, so you have time to ask before you commit. The questions below form a simple due-diligence checklist that protects your money and your legal standing.

### 1. What is the total upfront cost, and does it match the legal limits?

Ask for an itemized list of everything due at signing. In New York, the only legal upfront charges are the security deposit (capped at one month of rent) plus the first month of rent, with any application fee capped at $20. A reassuring answer matches these limits exactly and arrives in writing.

### 2. Who pays the broker fee, and who does the broker represent?

Under the FARE Act (June 2025), the broker fee falls on whoever hired the broker. Most renters who do not hire their own agent no longer pay it. Confirm plainly who the broker represents so you know whether any fee is yours to carry.

### 3. Is the unit rent stabilized?

Rent stabilization covers roughly 1,000,000 units and brings capped increases and a guaranteed right to renew. It is not shown in listings, so you have to ask. A good answer points you to the lease rider, and you can verify it by requesting a rent history from NY HCR.

### 4. Who pays the utilities (heat, gas, electric)?

Utilities are a recurring cost that listings rarely spell out. Ask specifically who covers heat, gas, and electric, since the split changes your real monthly budget. A clear answer names each utility and who is responsible for it.

### 5. Is there a laundry-room or amenity fee?

Some buildings charge separately for the laundry room or other shared amenities. These small recurring fees add up and are easy to overlook. Ask whether any amenity carries a charge so there are no surprises after you move in.

### 6. Is parking included or extra?

If you need parking, confirm whether it is part of the rent or a separate monthly cost. This is another recurring expense worth pinning down before you sign. A reassuring answer states the cost in plain terms or confirms it is included.

### 7. Will I be a named tenant on the lease?

Being a named tenant on the lease is what secures your legal protections. If your name is not on the document, your standing is far weaker. Confirm that you appear on the lease as a tenant, not merely as an occupant.

### 8. How are repairs and the superintendent handled?

Ask how repair requests are made and who the superintendent is. Knowing the process before you sign tells you how the building treats its tenants day to day. A good answer gives you a clear point of contact and a sense of how requests are tracked.

### 9. What is the policy on pets, guests, and subletting?

Lease policies on pets, guests, and subletting vary widely, and they shape how you actually live in the space. Read these clauses carefully and ask about anything ambiguous. A reassuring answer is written into the lease, not just promised aloud.

### 10. What are the lease length and renewal terms?

Understand how long the lease runs and what happens at renewal. If the unit is rent stabilized, renewal is guaranteed with capped increases. For other units, ask how renewal and any increase would work so you can plan ahead.

### 11. Can I review the full lease in advance and get a signed copy?

You should always get time to review the lease before signing and a fully executed copy afterward. A landlord who welcomes your questions and provides the document is a good sign. Keep your signed copy somewhere safe for the life of the tenancy.

## Common questions

### Do I have to pay a broker fee in NYC?

Not always. Since the FARE Act took effect in June 2025, the fee falls on whoever hired the broker, so most renters who did not hire their own agent no longer pay it.

### How do I know if an apartment is rent stabilized?

Ask the landlord, check the lease for a stabilization rider, and request a rent history from NY HCR. Stabilization is not listed publicly, so confirming it is on you.
